No relevant publicly indexed federal case, public Texas Attorney General enforcement action, FTC proceeding, or Save This Life–Covetrus/vendor lawsuit was found. These are limited negative results, not proof that no nonpublic complaint or unindexed case exists.

Under Texas Business Organizations Code Sections 11.356 and 11.357, the governing persons serving when a filing entity is terminated continue to manage its affairs for a limited three-year period for purposes such as holding and liquidating property and settling unfinished affairs. Therefore, the directors serving on the actual effective termination date are the leading candidates to authorize a wind-up transaction involving company assets. [Texas Business Organizations Code, Chapter 11](https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov/Docs/BO/htm/BO.11.htm)

Christian R. White and Lucy B. Hope are candidates for that role, but they cannot yet be treated as confirmed current transfer authorities. Certified public-information reports, the exact forfeiture instrument/date, the 2021 amendment, and a lien search must first establish who governed at termination and whether another party has superior rights.

This is a research assessment, not legal advice.

## 3. Texas forfeiture and wind-up authority

Texas Tax Code Chapter 171 permits forfeiture of corporate privileges for failures involving franchise-tax reports, payments, or examination of records. The Secretary of State may later forfeit the charter or certificate after the statutory process. [Texas Tax Code, Chapter 171](https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov/Docs/TX/pdf/TX.171.pdf)

Texas Business Organizations Code Chapter 11 provides that:

- a terminated filing entity survives for three years for limited purposes, including claims, liquidating property, distributing proceeds, and settling unfinished affairs;
- governing persons serving at termination retain powers necessary for those limited purposes;
- the entity may not simply resume ordinary business without reinstatement;
- a court may supervise winding up or appoint someone to conduct it.

### Preliminary authority assessment

1. **Most likely voluntary transfer authority:** The directors/governing persons serving on the effective termination date, acting within a lawful wind-up and subject to liabilities, contracts, privacy commitments, and liens.
2. **Possible court-controlled authority:** A court-appointed receiver or other person appointed to supervise winding up.
3. **Possible asset-specific rights:** A secured creditor may have enforcement rights in its collateral but does not automatically become the corporation's general wind-up authority.
4. **Not sufficient by itself:** Registered-agent status, an officer title without board authority, historical ownership, technical custody, possession of a backup, or control of a vendor account.

If the effective termination occurred during 2024, the ordinary three-year limited-survival period may extend into 2027. The exact termination date must be obtained before relying on this inference.

## 4. UCC and secured-creditor search

No indexed Texas UCC filing, secured creditor, SBA/PPP lien, or comparable security record tied to Save This Life was located through public web searching.

This does not support a no-lien conclusion because:

- Texas's authoritative UCC debtor search is paid and name-sensitive;

Texas discontinued paper UCC filing submissions on August 29, 2025, so the current SOS UCC Portal/account workflow—not a mailed UCC11 form—must be used. ### Materials not recovered

The operative historical Save This Life privacy policy, full registration terms, clinic agreements, vendor agreements, and data-transfer provisions were not located in a reliable public copy. The surviving Shopify `/policies/privacy-policy` endpoint currently returns a 404 page, not a policy.

Consequently, Phase 1 cannot establish:

- whether the company promised never to sell or transfer personal information;
- whether a merger, asset-sale, or successor-transfer clause existed;
- whether clinics or owners granted consent to a successor registry;
- whether specific vendors were controllers, processors, or independent recipients;
- what deletion, retention, security, or breach-notification promises applied.

These terms are a mandatory due-diligence item. FTC precedent shows that a failed company's privacy promises can constrain transfer of customer data, particularly when personal information is proposed for sale as a standalone asset. [FTC Toysmart settlement](https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/news/press-releases/2000/07/ftc-announces-settlement-bankrupt-website-toysmartcom-regarding-alleged-privacy-policy-violations), [FTC Borders guidance](https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/news/press-releases/2011/09/ftc-seeks-protection-personal-customer-information-borders-bankruptcy-proceeding)
